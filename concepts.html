Orbital Mechanics Concepts

Understanding the 15 Critical Elements in ODIN Trajectory Planning

Physics-Based Trajectory Planning

ODIN's trajectory planning system relies on 15 fundamental orbital elements that determine both initial planning and subsequent replanning of satellite trajectories. Each element plays a critical role in ensuring physically valid, safe, and efficient spacecraft operations.

Classical Orbital Elements (6)

The six Keplerian elements that uniquely define any satellite's orbit

1. Semi-major Axis (a)

a

Definition: Defines the size of the orbit, determining total orbital energy needed.

Impact: Directly affects transfer times between Earth and Moon and fuel (Δv) requirements for trajectory changes.

ODIN Usage: Calculates feasible paths and optimizes orbit insertion and departure burns.

2. Eccentricity (e)

e

Definition: Dictates orbit shape from circular to elliptical; more eccentric orbits have varying speeds.

Impact: Affects timing of burns and spacecraft velocity at different orbital positions.

ODIN Usage: Times burns correctly for orbital adjustments and hazard avoidance at specific orbital radii.

3. Inclination (i)

i

Definition: Orbital tilt against Earth's equatorial plane, governing accessible space regions.

Impact: Changes involve high Δv costs due to plane changes.

ODIN Usage: Weighs fuel costs versus mission benefits when adjusting inclination.

4. Longitude of Ascending Node (Ω)

Ω

Definition: Horizontal orientation where orbit crosses Earth's equatorial plane.

Impact: Critical for aligning spacecraft with targets or avoiding debris corridors.

ODIN Usage: Adjusts orientation to optimize rendezvous and hazard avoidance.

5. Argument of Periapsis (ω)

ω

Definition: Specifies where the orbit's closest point occurs; timing maneuvers here maximizes efficiency.

Impact: Optimal burn timing for maximum efficiency.

ODIN Usage: Schedules burns and hazard avoidance maneuvers for optimal safety and fuel use.

6. True Anomaly (ν)

ν

Definition: Spacecraft's exact position along orbit at any time.

Impact: Essential for real-time tracking and decision-making.

ODIN Usage: Constantly updates to monitor spacecraft status relative to hazards or trajectory goals.

Anomaly Elements (2)

Mathematical angles for precise position calculation and orbital propagation

7. Mean Anomaly (M)

M

Definition: Average position used for orbital propagation.

Impact: Enables prediction of spacecraft's future orbital location.

ODIN Usage: Predicts spacecraft's future location in orbit for replanning algorithms.

8. Eccentric Anomaly (E)

E

Definition: Auxiliary mathematical angle for position calculation.

Impact: Speeds up trajectory propagation calculations.

ODIN Usage: Enables rapid updates critical for real-time trajectory planning.

Dynamic & Kinematic Elements (3)

Energy and momentum considerations for physically valid maneuvers

9. Orbital Energy

E

Definition: Sum of kinetic and potential energy; governs transfer feasibility.

Impact: Determines delta-v needed for maneuvering.

ODIN Usage: Prioritizes trajectories that conserve fuel while meeting time and safety requirements.

10. Angular Momentum

h

Definition: Conserved in absence of external forces; affects orbit shape.

Impact: Determines how maneuvers alter trajectory.

ODIN Usage: Factors momentum conservation for realistic, physically allowable maneuvers.

11. Flight Path Angle

γ

Definition: Angle of spacecraft velocity to local horizon.

Impact: Critical during burns for accurate injection or transfer phases.

ODIN Usage: Calculates and controls for safe and efficient trajectory transfers.

Relative Motion Elements (1)

Velocity relationships for collision avoidance and rendezvous

12. Relative Velocity Vectors

v⃗ᵣₑₗ

Definition: Velocity difference relative to targets, hazards, or debris.

Impact: Essential for collision avoidance and rendezvous planning.

ODIN Usage: Analyzes continuously to trigger real-time replanning if threats detected.

Environmental & Temporal Elements (3)

External factors affecting orbital mechanics and mission planning

13. Orbital Plane Orientation/Rotation

Ω̇

Definition: Spatial direction of the orbit and its rotation rate.

Impact: Adjustments can avoid hazardous space weather or debris clusters.

ODIN Usage: Evaluates plane orientation shifts to minimize exposure risks while considering fuel penalties.

14. Perigee/Apogee Altitude

rₚ/rₐ

Definition: Closest and farthest orbit points relative to Earth.

Impact: Governs interaction with environmental hazards like radiation belts or debris fields.

ODIN Usage: Plans altitude adjustments to avoid hazardous zones or optimize mission profiles.

15. Epoch/Time Stamp

t₀

Definition: Reference time for all orbital parameters and state vectors.

Impact: Ensures synchronization of physical models with real-time data.

ODIN Usage: Enables accurate forward propagation and validation against current environmental conditions.

Physics Validation in ODIN Path Selection

Every trajectory generated by ODIN undergoes rigorous physics-based validation to ensure feasibility, safety, and efficiency.

1

Trajectory Simulation

Every candidate path is simulated using Newton's equations of motion and gravity, solved numerically to ensure the spacecraft's predicted position and velocity are physically possible given current mass, thrust, and momentum.

2

Fuel and Thrust Constraints

Physics-based checks use the rocket equation, guaranteeing maneuvers can be executed without exceeding fuel or propulsion limits. All Δv requirements are validated against available resources.

3

Collision and Hazard Checking

For each new path, physics-driven algorithms calculate probability of impact with debris and exposures to radiation zones using real-time models. Infeasible or unsafe paths are immediately discarded.

4

Dynamic Constraint Verification

Optimized trajectories are validated by simulating forward propagation. Paths must maintain conservation laws (momentum, energy) and respect planetary gravitational fields for proper transfer orbits.

5

Experimental and Numerical Validation

Solutions are cross-checked with other numerical tools and simulation models. Advanced AI systems use physics-informed neural networks trained to generate only physically-valid solutions.

Fundamental Physics Principles

Kepler's Laws

Govern planetary motion and orbital mechanics, ensuring all trajectories follow natural celestial mechanics rather than arbitrary paths.

Newton's Laws

Define how forces affect motion, ensuring all proposed maneuvers respect fundamental physics of acceleration and momentum.

Conservation Laws

Energy and momentum conservation ensure all trajectory changes are physically realizable and maintain orbital stability.

Rocket Equation

Tsiolkovsky's equation validates that all Δv requirements can be met with available fuel mass and propulsion systems.

Perturbation Theory

Accounts for real-world forces like atmospheric drag, solar pressure, and gravitational perturbations in trajectory planning.

Orbital Mechanics

Six orbital elements uniquely define any orbit, providing the mathematical framework for both planning and replanning.
